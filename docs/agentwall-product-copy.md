# Agentwall Product Copy Draft

## Homepage hero
**Put agents in production without giving them a free pass to your network, tools, browser, or secrets.**

## Subhead
Agentwall is a provenance-aware enforcement layer for AI agents. It evaluates outbound actions before execution, applies default-deny policy across the real-world surfaces that matter, and gives operators a structured audit trail for every allow, deny, and approval decision.

## 5 concise landing-page bullets
- **Control actions, not just prompts** — enforce policy on network egress, tool use, browser actions, secret access, and content flows.
- **Default-deny by design** — block private ranges, metadata endpoints, and unapproved outbound destinations unless an operator explicitly allows them.
- **See what caused the action** — use provenance and trust labels to distinguish user input, web content, tool output, memory, and derived data.
- **Keep humans in the loop when it counts** — route risky actions into approval instead of choosing between blind autonomy and total shutdown.
- **Get operator-grade auditability** — emit structured decisions your team can inspect, review, and wire into incident response workflows.

## Short positioning paragraph
Agentwall is for teams that want to keep their existing agent stack but add a real control plane between models and the outside world. It does not ask you to migrate your prompts, retrain your workflows, or buy into a new all-in-one platform. It sits in front of the risky edges — egress, tools, browser automation, secrets, and governance actions — so operators can enforce policy, require approval where needed, and understand exactly why an agent tried to act.

## Why not migrate platforms?
A lot of enterprise AI products promise safety by asking you to move everything into their platform. That's a big swap: new abstractions, new runtime constraints, new procurement, new retraining, and usually a lot less control than the demo implies.

Agentwall takes the opposite view. If you're already building with your own agent framework, internal tools, or orchestration layer — or evaluating platform-heavy options like NemoClaw — the fastest path is usually **not** a migration. It's adding enforcement where risk actually appears.

Why teams choose that route:
- **Lower switching cost** — keep your current models, tools, and workflows instead of rebuilding them inside someone else's product.
- **More realistic security** — control real actions at execution time rather than relying on platform-level promises and prompt hygiene.
- **Faster operator adoption** — security and platform teams can review concrete policy decisions, approvals, and audit logs without waiting for a full re-platform.
- **Less lock-in** — your control layer remains useful even as models, frameworks, and internal agents change.
- **Better for mixed environments** — one enforcement layer can cover homegrown agents, wrappers, and future integrations instead of forcing a single platform standard.

If NemoClaw or another enterprise platform is the right fit for workflow management, fine — but platform migration and action enforcement are different decisions. Agentwall is for teams that want the enforcement layer now, without betting the whole stack on a platform switch.
